How Much Does a Restaurant Website Cost in 2026?

By AIWebGrade Team·July 11, 2026·8 min read

Prices range from free to $8,000+, and the gap isn't random — each tier gets you a genuinely different set of features. Here's what you're actually paying for at each level.

"How much should a restaurant website cost" doesn't have one answer because a restaurant website isn't one product. A single-page menu site and a full ordering-and-reservations platform solve completely different problems, and pricing them the same way would be misleading. Here's the honest breakdown by tier.

$0-500DIY builder tier
$800-2,500professional template tier
$2,500-8,000+custom AI-powered tier

Tier 1: DIY builders — $0 to $500

Wix, Squarespace, and similar tools let an owner build a basic site themselves for the cost of a monthly plan. This works for a single-location restaurant that just needs hours, menu, and contact info visible. The tradeoffs: generic templates that look like thousands of other restaurant sites, limited or clunky online ordering integrations, and slower load speeds than purpose-built sites — which matters more than it sounds, since a slow menu page loses hungry customers to whichever competitor loads first.

Tier 2: Professional template builds — $800 to $2,500

A freelancer or small agency customizes a premium template with your branding, photography, and menu. This tier typically includes proper mobile optimization, basic SEO setup, and integration with a third-party ordering platform like Toast or Square. It's the right tier for most single-location, established restaurants that want to look professional without the cost of a fully custom build.

Tier 3: Custom AI-powered builds — $2,500 to $8,000+

This tier includes custom design (not a template), built-in reservation and ordering systems rather than third-party redirects, AI chat for instant answers to hours/menu/allergen questions, and often multi-location support for restaurant groups. It's the right tier for restaurants doing meaningful online order volume, where a few extra percentage points of conversion translate into real revenue — and where a chatbot answering "are you open on Christmas" at 11pm captures an order that would otherwise be lost. This is the tier Brivex AI tends to recommend once a restaurant's own numbers show the ordering volume to justify it — not before, since a $6,000 build makes no sense for a place doing 15 online orders a week.

📊 Where the extra cost pays for itself

  • A template site converts online menu views to orders at roughly 1-2%
  • A custom site with built-in ordering and instant chat converts at roughly 3-5%
  • At 3,000 monthly menu views and a $35 average order, that gap is worth $1,700-$3,000/month
  • A $5,000 custom build often pays for itself within 2-3 months at meaningful order volume

What actually drives the price within each tier

Number of locations, whether online ordering is built-in versus linked out, whether reservations sync with a system like OpenTable or are custom-built, photography (professional food photography alone often runs $500-1,500), and ongoing content updates for seasonal menus all move the price within a tier more than the tier itself does. A quick audit of your current site will usually tell you which of these is actually worth paying for next, rather than upgrading everything at once and hoping it pays off.

"The most expensive restaurant website isn't the one with the highest price tag. It's the cheap one that loses a table booking every night because it's slow, confusing, or looks unfinished."

⚠️ Watch for hidden ongoing costs. Some "cheap" builds carry $50-200/month platform fees that add up to more than a custom site over three years. Always ask for the 3-year total cost, not just the build price.

Common questions

Is a free website builder good enough for a restaurant?

A free builder can work as a temporary placeholder, but most lack real online ordering integration, load slowly, and look generic — all of which cost more in lost reservations and orders than the money saved on the build itself.

Do I need online ordering built into the site or is a third-party link enough?

A third-party link works but sends customers off-site and often adds a service fee they notice. Ordering built directly into your website keeps the experience seamless and keeps more margin, though it does raise the build cost.

How much should ongoing maintenance cost after launch?

Budget $30 to $150 a month depending on how often the menu changes and whether the site includes booking or ordering integrations that need occasional updates and monitoring.
