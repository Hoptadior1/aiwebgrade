The E-commerce Website Guide: Where the 70% of Carts Actually Go

By AIWebGrade Team·July 6, 2026·8 min read

70% of carts are abandoned before purchase — not because shoppers changed their mind, but because something specific in the checkout flow made them stop. The fix is usually smaller than owners assume.

Cart abandonment is treated as a mysterious, unavoidable cost of doing business online. In practice, most of it traces back to a small number of specific, fixable friction points that happen at a predictable moment in the checkout process — usually the exact instant an unexpected cost or requirement appears.

Unlike a service business where a lead can still be recovered through follow-up, an abandoned cart on an e-commerce site rarely converts through a phone call. The moment is gone, and the shopper has usually already opened a competitor's product page in another tab.

Where the abandonment actually happens

Shipping cost revealed too late

Unexpected shipping cost at the final checkout step is consistently cited as the single largest cause of cart abandonment. Displaying shipping cost — or a clear threshold for free shipping — earlier in the browsing experience prevents the sense of being ambushed at the last step.

Account creation required before purchase

Forcing account creation before checkout adds friction that a meaningful share of shoppers simply won't tolerate. A guest checkout option, with account creation offered afterward, captures sales that would otherwise be lost to impatience.

Checkout taking too many steps

Every additional page or field in checkout increases the chance of abandonment. Consolidating checkout to the fewest possible steps — ideally one page — measurably improves completion rate compared to a multi-page process.

"Most abandoned carts aren't lost sales — they're sales that were nearly won and then lost to a specific, identifiable moment of friction. Fixing that moment recovers real revenue without spending a dollar more on traffic."

Speed matters more here than almost any other industry

A 7% conversion drop per extra second of load time compounds directly with cart abandonment — a slow product page loses shoppers before they even reach checkout, and a slow checkout loses the ones who made it that far. Performance optimisation is not a technical nicety for e-commerce; it's directly tied to revenue.

Reviews displayed at the point of decision

Product reviews placed directly on the product page, not buried in a separate tab, reduce hesitation exactly when a shopper is deciding whether to add an item to cart. The closer the proof is to the decision point, the more effectively it works. Reviews mentioning specific product details, not generic praise, tend to carry the most persuasive weight.

Trust badges matter more for first-time buyers

Security badges, clear return policy links, and recognisable payment icons displayed near the checkout button reduce hesitation for a shopper buying from your store for the first time, when trust in an unfamiliar brand is still being established.

A quick self-check

Add an item to your own cart and go through checkout as a first-time customer would. Count the steps, note when shipping cost first appears, and check whether an account is required. Each friction point found is a specific, fixable reason your abandonment rate may be higher than it needs to be.

What good actually looks like

A well-optimised store shows shipping cost early, offers guest checkout, keeps the process to a single page, and loads product pages in under two seconds — each element individually small, but together responsible for a meaningfully lower abandonment rate.

Common questions

Does offering free shipping always help?

Building shipping cost into the product price and offering free shipping generally converts better than displaying a separate shipping charge, even when the total cost to the customer is identical — the psychology of a surprise charge matters more than the actual number.

Should I use a third-party checkout platform?

Established platforms like Shopify's native checkout are generally well-optimised already. The bigger risk is a custom checkout that hasn't been properly tested for speed and simplicity across devices.

How much does cart abandonment email recovery help?

A well-timed abandoned cart email sequence recovers a meaningful share of otherwise lost sales, but it works best alongside fixing the underlying friction — recovery emails treat the symptom, not the cause.

Does site speed matter more on product pages or checkout?

Both, but a slow checkout is more costly since the shopper has already invested time deciding to buy. A slow product page loses browsers before intent forms; a slow checkout loses shoppers who were already sold.
