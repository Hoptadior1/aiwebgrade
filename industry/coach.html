The Coach Website Guide: Giving Prospects Something Specific to Remember

By AIWebGrade Team·July 7, 2026·8 min read

88% of prospects check a coach's website before booking a call, often comparing several coaches with similar-sounding transformation promises. The one whose approach they can actually describe afterward usually wins.

Coaching is a crowded category where most websites say some version of the same thing — unlock your potential, transform your life, achieve your goals. This language is emotionally appealing but functionally useless for a prospect trying to distinguish one coach from another, because every competitor's site says something nearly identical.

A prospect comparing multiple coaches before a discovery call needs something concrete to differentiate you — a specific, nameable methodology, a clear description of what actually happens in a session, or a defined outcome tied to a specific timeframe. Vague inspiration doesn't survive a side-by-side comparison; specifics do.

What actually differentiates a coaching website

A named, specific methodology

Rather than generic language about mindset and growth, a specific framework — even one you've named yourself — gives prospects something concrete to evaluate and remember. It also signals that your approach is systematic rather than improvised session to session.

A clear description of what a session actually involves

Many coaching websites describe outcomes extensively but say almost nothing about the actual process — is it weekly calls, homework between sessions, a group component. Prospects hesitant about coaching in general respond well to demystifying exactly what they're signing up for.

Specific, verifiable client outcomes

A testimonial naming a specific result — "increased revenue by a defined amount within six months" — carries more weight than a vague quote about feeling more confident, even when both are genuine. Specificity is what makes a testimonial persuasive rather than just pleasant.

"A prospect comparing three coaches with almost identical mission statements will remember whichever one gave them something concrete to hold onto — a named framework, a specific process, a defined result. Everything else blurs together."

The discovery call booking link needs to be everywhere

Many coaching sites bury their calendar link at the bottom of a long page. A prospect who has just been persuaded by a specific piece of content — a case study, a methodology explanation — should be able to book immediately from that same page, not hunt for a separate contact section.

Video content builds trust faster than text alone

A short, genuine video of the coach explaining their approach personally often converts better than pages of written copy, since coaching is an inherently personal relationship and prospects want a sense of who they'd actually be working with before committing to a call.

Group programs need different proof than one-on-one coaching

If you offer both formats, the website should distinguish clearly between them — group program testimonials should reflect the cohort experience specifically, while one-on-one testimonials should emphasise the personalised attention that format is meant to provide.

A quick self-check

Read your own homepage and try to summarise your specific methodology in one sentence to someone unfamiliar with your business. If you can't, a prospect comparing you against other coaches likely can't either — and will default to whichever coach made this easier to grasp.

What good actually looks like

A well-built coaching website names a specific methodology, shows a booking link on every page rather than one buried contact section, and backs claims with quantified client outcomes — giving prospects something concrete to compare against every other coach's site.

Common questions

Should pricing be shown publicly?

At minimum, a general price range reduces friction. Complete silence on cost is one of the more common reasons a prospect never books a discovery call at all, assuming the worst without asking rather than simply reaching out to find out.

How specific should client testimonials be?

As specific as confidentiality allows — a defined outcome, timeframe, or starting situation makes a testimonial genuinely persuasive rather than generic praise every coaching site includes.

Is a free resource or lead magnet worth building?

Yes — a genuinely useful free guide or assessment captures prospects who aren't ready to book a call yet, keeping them in your funnel through email rather than losing them entirely to a competitor's more immediate offer.
