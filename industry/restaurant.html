🍽️ RESTAURANTS WEBSITE GUIDE

The Restaurant Website Guide: Your Menu Photos Are Doing All the Selling

By AIWebGrade Team·July 6, 2026·8 min read

A hungry visitor scrolling your menu makes a decision in seconds based almost entirely on what they see. If the photos load slowly, that decision has already been made — and it wasn't in your favour.

89% of diners check a restaurant's photos or reviews before deciding where to eat, often while standing outside comparing two or three nearby options on their phone. This is one of the few industries where the decision genuinely happens in the moment, on the sidewalk, based on what loads on a small screen in the next few seconds.

A restaurant website is unusually visual, and unusually judged in real time. Unlike a service business where a prospect might browse calmly at a desk, a restaurant visitor is often hungry, comparing live, and has almost zero patience for a slow-loading gallery.

Why restaurant sites load so slowly, specifically

Menu photography tends to be shot at high resolution and uploaded without compression — a single dish photo straight from a professional camera can be 20 times larger than what a phone screen actually needs to display it well. Multiply that across a full menu page and load times routinely stretch past 6 seconds.

The specific fixes that matter most here

Compress every menu photo without visible quality loss

Bringing images down to under 200KB each, using modern formats like WebP, typically cuts load time dramatically without a diner noticing any difference in image quality on a phone screen.

Load the hero image first, everything else after

The single most important image — the one representing your best dish or your dining room — should load before anything else, since that's what forms the first impression while the rest of the page catches up.

Make the booking action impossible to miss

Whether it's a phone number, a reservation widget, or a delivery link, the next step should be visible without scrolling. A visitor who has to hunt for how to book a table often just tries the next restaurant instead.

"For a restaurant, the website isn't a description of the experience — for those crucial first seconds, it functionally is the experience. A slow, ugly gallery creates the same hesitation a messy dining room would in person."

Reviews function differently here than in other industries

Restaurant reviews are read quickly and specifically — diners scan for mentions of a particular dish, wait times, or atmosphere rather than reading in depth. Recent reviews mentioning specific, appealing details often carry more weight than a high star average with generic comments.

A quick self-check

Open your own menu page on mobile data, not wifi, and time how long the first photo takes to appear. If it's more than two seconds, a hungry visitor comparing you against a nearby competitor has likely already tapped away.

Common questions

Should I show full pricing on the website?

Yes — hiding pricing or requiring a click-through to see it adds friction that this fast-decision visitor rarely tolerates. Clear, visible pricing next to each dish photo removes a common reason for hesitation.

Is a reservation widget worth the added page weight?

Generally yes, provided it's implemented efficiently — the convenience of booking without leaving the page typically outweighs a small increase in load time, as long as the widget itself isn't poorly optimised.

How many menu photos is too many?

A curated set of your best 15-20 dishes, loading quickly, outperforms a full photographic menu that's slow and overwhelming. Diners want to feel the vibe and see the standouts, not review an exhaustive catalogue.

What about video content — worth the extra load time?

A short looped video of the dining room atmosphere can be effective, but only after the essential photos and booking information load first. Video should enhance the page, never delay the moment a hungry visitor sees what they came for.

Does the website even matter if we're mostly booked through delivery apps?

Yes — delivery platforms take a commission on every order, while your own website converts orders at no per-transaction cost. A fast, well-designed site pointing customers to order directly protects margin that delivery apps otherwise capture.
